## Issues to fix

- **CAR figures (38–75%, ~30–45 min) — MISMATCH on primary citation.** PMC8813037 does NOT contain these numbers. Cite the figures to **Wüst et al. 2000** ("38% and 75%", peak "~30 minutes after awakening") and **Fries et al. 2009** (≈77% prevalence) directly, not to the cortisol review or Wikipedia. Also correct the window: primary source says ~30 min, so "~30–45 min" is APPROXIMATE — tighten to ~30 min (≤45 min at most).
- **PMC8813037 author/journal — MISMATCH.** Cited as "Clow & Smyth, *Compr Physiol*"; the article is **O'Byrne NA, Yuen F, Butt WZ, Liu PY (2021), "Sleep and Circadian Regulation of Cortisol: A Short Review," *Curr Opin Endocr Metab Res* 18:178–186.** Fix author and journal (title is actually correct).
- **SLEEP/DLMO author — MISMATCH.** Cited "Crowley et al."; sole author is **David J. Kennaway (2023, *Sleep* 46(5), zsad033).** Fix author.
- **PMC3588003 author — MISMATCH + MOVED.** Cited "Chellappa et al."; authors are **Wahnschaffe et al. (2013).** Title and claim are fine. Update URL to https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3588003/ .
- **PMC9541543 — APPROXIMATE.** Reichert et al. 2022 is more cautious than the doc implies (adenosine "unlikely responsible for the diurnal changes…under undisturbed baseline conditions"). Soften the unqualified Process-S claim or lean on PMC4810168 for it.
- **Nature s41598-024-67297-y author — MISMATCH + MOVED.** Cited "Thomas et al."; authors are **Windred et al. (2024), *Sci Rep* 14:16796.** Nature link hits an idp.nature.com auth redirect for bots; use the free PMC mirror **https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11263371/** . Note: the specific "nadir 3–5 AM" detail is general circadian background, not the subject of this temperature-amplitude/metabolomics paper.
- **PMID 8877121 (Monk) window — APPROXIMATE.** Monk's test interval is described as 10:00–15:00, not 14:00–16:00. The 14:00–16:00 figure is supported by PMC6430172 (Valdez); keep Valdez as the citation for that specific window.
- **PMC6685797 (post-lunch light) — APPROXIMATE / partial MISMATCH.** Askaripoor et al. found light improved EEG alertness correlates and mood (red light) but NOT subjective alertness or task performance. Revise claim to "improves physiological/EEG correlates of alertness (and mood under some conditions), though not subjective alertness or task performance."
- **PMC1408021 author — MISMATCH.** Cited "Sohn & Lam"; article is **Lam RW & Levitan RD (2000), "Pathophysiology of seasonal affective disorder: a review," *J Psychiatry Neurosci* 25(5):469–480.** Content supports the claim; fix author.
- **PMC6422319 (light therapy "established, effective") — MISMATCH.** Source is the **Nussbaumer-Streit et al. (2019) Cochrane review on PREVENTING SAD**, which found only one small high-bias study and concluded evidence is insufficient to draw conclusions. It does NOT establish light therapy as effective. Either replace with a treatment-efficacy review/meta-analysis (e.g., a Cochrane review of light therapy for treating SAD) or soften the claim to reflect that prevention evidence is limited.
- **PMID 36794795 (Lynott) — APPROXIMATE.** This meta-analysis found NO reliable temperature→behaviour effect. Acceptable only as the contrasting weak/lab evidence; do not let it read as support for the heat→aggression effect. The primary support for the heat hypothesis is PMC11477092 (Choi et al.).
- **MOVED (non-substantive 301 redirects, update for cleanliness):** PMC3588003, PMC10558889, PMC12617017, PMC6510434 — all `www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/...` → `pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/...`.
