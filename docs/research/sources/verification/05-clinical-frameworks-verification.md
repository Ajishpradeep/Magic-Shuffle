## Issues to fix

- **MISMATCH — schizophrenia dosage citation, wrong authors.** Reference for PubMed 27192343 reads "Carr, Odell-Miller & Priebe / dosage in schizophrenia — *Influence of Dosage and Type of Music Therapy* (Issues Ment Health Nurs, 2016)" and §2 attributes the dosage claim to "schizophrenia dosage review (Carr/Gold)." PubMed 27192343 is actually **Chung J & Woods-Giscombe C (2016)**, *Influence of Dosage and Type of Music Therapy in Symptom Management and Rehabilitation for Individuals With Schizophrenia* (Issues Ment Health Nurs 37(8):611–623). Correction: change the cited authors to **Chung & Woods-Giscombe**. (Carr/Gold/Odell-Miller/Priebe are real music-therapy-dosage authors but on *different* papers — if you specifically want a Carr/Gold dosage source, cite it separately with its own DOI; do not attach their names to this PMID.)

- **MISMATCH/correction — RAS Parkinson's "cadence" overstated.** §1.2 / ref Frontiers fneur.2022.940419 lists "speed, stride length, **cadence**" as significantly improved. That meta-analysis found stride length and gait speed significant but **step frequency (cadence) NOT significant**. Correction: drop "cadence" from the Parkinson's-improvement list (or note it as non-significant); cadence *is* significant in the stroke review (PMC9714437, MD 5.16), so the claim holds for stroke but not PD.

- **MISMATCH/caveat — cortisol PMC11673928 supports the opposite direction.** §6 cites PMC11673928 as "salivary cortisol as objective stress response to music; reliably falls with music." The actual 2024 study shows loud favorite music *raises* cortisol (a stress *response*), with subjects unaware. It is a valid hearing-safety/stress-marker citation but does **not** support "cortisol reliably falls." Correction: either re-cite it under §5.5 (hearing safety) framing, or replace it with a study actually showing cortisol reduction for the §3/§6 "cortisol down" claim.

- **APPROXIMATE — de Witte 2020 "selection method NS moderator / 60–80 BPM non-lyrical drives physiological effect."** The paper (Health Psychology Review 14(2)) is real and the headline effects (physiological d=.380, psychological d=.545, outcome-type moderation) are confirmed, but the specific sub-claims about selection method being a non-significant moderator and the 60–80 BPM/non-lyrical/controlled-intensity recipe driving the physiological effect could not be confirmed from the open abstract. Verify against the full text before presenting as a hard finding.

- **APPROXIMATE — "~5% musical anhedonia."** Mas-Herrero/Martínez-Molina 2016 PNAS states **3–5%** specific musical anhedonia. The doc's "~5%" is the high end of the range. Minor; consider stating "~3–5%." Also note the lead author is Martínez-Molina (Mas-Herrero is second author) — current "Mas-Herrero et al." is acceptable shorthand.

- **MOVED (non-substantive) — two PMC links use the legacy host.** `ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9714437/` and `.../PMC9690210/` 301-redirect to `pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/...`. They resolve fine; optionally update to the canonical `pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov` host for consistency with the other PMC links.
